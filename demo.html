Marbury v. Madison

5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 | Supreme Court of the United States | February 24, 1803
Relevant Facts
  • Adams (Federalist) loses presidency to Jefferson (Republican-Democrat) in the 1800 election
  • Before leaving office, Adams appoints 42 justices of the peace for D.C., including William Marbury
  • Marbury's commission is signed by the President and sealed by the Secretary of State (Marshall himself), but not delivered before Jefferson's inauguration
  • Jefferson's administration, through Secretary of State James Madison, refuses to deliver Marbury's commission
  • Marbury seeks a writ of mandamus directly from the Supreme Court to compel delivery
Procedural Posture
  • Marbury petitions the Supreme Court directly for a writ of mandamus under §13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789
  • The case arises under the Court's original jurisdiction (not appellate)
  • Supreme Court hears the case and issues its opinion
Legal Question(s) Presented
  1. Has Marbury a right to the commission he demands?
  2. If he has a right, and that right has been violated, do the laws of the country afford him a remedy?
  3. If they do afford him a remedy, is it a mandamus issuing from the Supreme Court?
  4. Does §13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789, which grants the Supreme Court original jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus, violate Article III of the Constitution?
Court's Decision
The Court held that while Marbury had a vested legal right to the commission and the laws did afford him a remedy, the Supreme Court could not issue the writ because §13 of the Judiciary Act unconstitutionally expanded the Court's original jurisdiction beyond what Article III permits.
Legal Principle(s)
  1. Judicial Review: It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.
  2. The Constitution is the "superior, paramount law" and ordinary statutes that conflict with it are void.
  3. Article III fixes the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction; Congress cannot enlarge it by statute.
  4. Congress may regulate only the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction, not its original jurisdiction.
Court's Analysis

Marshall structured the opinion in three parts:

1. Right to the Commission

  • The appointment was complete when the commission was signed and sealed
  • Delivery was merely a ministerial act, not a discretionary one
  • Therefore, Marbury had a vested legal right

2. Remedy Exists

  • Where there's a legal right, there's a legal remedy
  • The violation of a vested legal right is a wrong that courts can redress

3. The Supreme Court Cannot Provide This Remedy

  • Article III §2 defines original jurisdiction narrowly: cases affecting ambassadors, other public ministers, and those in which a state is a party
  • §13 of the Judiciary Act attempts to grant original jurisdiction for writs of mandamus
  • This conflicts with Article III and is therefore unconstitutional
  • The Court cannot enforce an unconstitutional statute, even to provide justice in a particular case
Concurring Opinion(s)

None

Dissenting Opinion(s)

None

Importance & Impact
  • Established the doctrine of judicial review — the Supreme Court's authority to declare acts of Congress unconstitutional
  • Transformed the judiciary into a co-equal branch of government
  • Created the framework for constitutional interpretation that persists today
  • One of the most cited and foundational decisions in American law
  • Marshall's political genius: chastised Jefferson's administration while avoiding a direct confrontation the Court would lose
Additional Notes
  • Political Context: Marshall was Adams's Secretary of State who had sealed the commissions. The decision allowed him to criticize Jefferson without issuing an order Jefferson would ignore.
  • Irony: Marbury never got his commission, but the case established the Court's power.
  • Marshall's Strategy: Found the plaintiffs were right on the merits but wrong on jurisdiction — a way to have his cake and eat it too.
  • Still taught as the first case in virtually every constitutional law course in the United States.
